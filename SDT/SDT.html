Signal Detection Theory
(In A Nutsell)

Signal Detection Theory (SDT) allows researchers to measure the abilities of people to detect a stimulus when presented among randomness or, as it is called in signal detection theory, noise. This broad definition allows for SDT to be applied to scenarios such as testing whether an x-ray is normal or abnormal or whether an earthquake is occurring or not.

Key Measurements in SDT

All statstics that are used in signal detection theory come from four basic measurements:

Hit

When a signal is present and the participant says the signal is present

Miss

When a signal is present but the participant says the signal is not present

False Alarm

When only noise is present but the participant says the signal is present

Correct Rejection

When only noise is present and the participant says there is no signal

Distributions of Signal and Noise

The analysis of a signal detection experiment relies on assuming a distribution of signal events and noise events. What this means is there are seperate probabilities of experiencing a signal event or a noise event. The distribution curves below are representative of theoretical noise and signal events. The higher the point on a distribution, the more likely it will occur.

Wickens, T. D. (2002). Elementary signal detection theory. Oxford University Press. Page 12

Now, when we overlay the signal distribution curve and the noise distribution curve, we see some overlap in the middle. This is where a participant is more likely to rely on guessing.

The first little character on the x axis λ ,or lambda, is what we call our decision criteria. The decision criteria is established through data collection of a participant's hits, misses, correct rejections, and false alarms. It's placement on a graph like the one below tells researchers what a participant's typical response behavior is statistically.

The other character on the x axis d' is known as sensitivity. d' measures how close the noise and signal distribution are to each other. The closer they are to each other, the harder it is to discriminate between noise and signal.

Wickens, T. D. (2002). Elementary signal detection theory. Oxford University Press. Page 13

Literature Review on Visual Search

In explaining our discussions to Dr. Liu, he told Jason and I our experiment would involve the topic of Visual Search. Visual search is a type of perceptual task typically involving an active scan of a visual environment for a particular object or feature (signal) among other objects (noise).

A literature review on visual search and visual search experiments pertaining to mobile devices was our next step. Below is an example of a visual search experiment offered through software from the University of Indiana Bloomington.

A common theme appearing in our research of visual search on mobile devices was the F-shaped pattern for text scanning. The F-shaped pattern appears when a user scans the first row of text thoroughly and each subsequent row below is less thourghly examined. The F-shape arises in people whose language reads from left to right.

The F-shape pattern was the key to forming our hypothesis:

Given typical web reading patterns, users are more sensitive to Quadrant 2 and 3 of a mobile interface, regardless of orientation.

Data Analysis

Jason and I conducted the experiment in our class of 11 other students, which is a very small sample size. The experiments were conducted on iMac Desktops in the UCLA psychology computer lab.

From the average hit rates, we see there are visibile differences in average hit rates across landscape modes and portrait modes. While these might be encouraging, we had to run an ANOVA (analysis of variance) to determine statstical validity of the hit rates. From the ANOVA test, we got found statstically differences between quadrants on different phone positions (P = 0.000000743).

Through post-hoc tests to find statistically significant differences between two groups, we found relevant, significant differences between the following quadrants:

  • Landscape Quadrant 2 vs Landscape Quadrant 4 (P = 0.00854)
  • Landscape Quadrant 2 vs Portrait Quadrant 2 (P = 0.03196)
  • Landscape Quadrant 3 vs Portrait Quadrant 3 (P = 0.00310)

Conclusion

Our hypothesis was correct in predicting user's would be more sensitive to quadrant 2 and quadrant 3, but only in portrait mode. From the post hoc tests, we saw statistically significant differences in the hit rates between quadrant 2 and quadrant 3 between landscape mode and portrait mode. In landscape mode, it seemed that quadrant 1 and quadrant 4 were more sensitive to detection.

While it should be noted that the sample size used in this experiment was quite limited, continued investigation into this topic could find similar results. While Jason and I's initial hypothesis was not proven, the notation that landscape mode and portrait mode have quadrant sensitivities could be of use to UX and UI designers. If more data is collected in experiments similar to this one, UX and UI should still use it on a case by case basis in their designs as to not interfere with aesthetic aspects of interfaces.
